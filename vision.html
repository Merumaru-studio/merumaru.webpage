Welcome — you made it to one of the coolest places on the web.

This is the website of a starting animation studio that uses… let’s just say unconventional software and hardware for 2026.

To explain how this happened, I need to tell a short story.

One day I came back from a long trip to Japan and just wanted to relax and watch my favorite TV show on my Apple TV. But I couldn’t — not because of Wi-Fi problems, but because Apple disabled access to TV shows I had already bought.

Why?

Because I usually charge my account using gift cards. I bought some of them in Japan, in a different currency, which caused my account to switch to the Japanese region. That meant I could no longer watch the shows I purchased overseas.

You’d think I could just switch it back, right?

Nope. To do that, I would have to spend all remaining money on the account — down to exactly 0 yen. I had 652 yen left. Basically impossible to spend perfectly. Support told me I should just connect a credit card to fix it.

And yes — I really didn’t want to do that. That’s literally why I use gift cards.

That was the moment where my thought process started.

Even if it sounds dramatic, I realized something simple: we used to own things.

I could buy a CD and listen to it forever. I could resell it. I could rip it to my iPod. Same with DVDs. And to some extent, software.

That’s mostly gone now.

Everything is a subscription. Everything depends on accounts, licenses, and internet access. We don’t really own our tools or media anymore — we just have permission to use them until someone decides otherwise.

As someone who wants to create digital worlds — as a 3D artist, animator, and game developer — that feels wrong. Creative tools should feel stable and dependable, not temporary.

I want to make people feel strong emotions. I want to tell stories and build worlds people care about. That part matters more than constantly chasing the newest tools.

Anyway — enough philosophy. Let’s talk about the project.

This project is mainly built around a short film idea I have (codename “Doggo”). It will mostly be animated in 3D.

Here’s where the practical problem starts: I’m still a student. I learned Autodesk Maya and really like working with it, but I only have a student license, which means I can’t use it commercially.

Some people will say: “Just use Blender.”

And yes — I know Blender is powerful, open-source, and free. But switching tools would mean a long learning phase before I can actually realize my idea. I also genuinely like Maya’s workflow and UI. That’s subjective, but it matters.

The other problem is hardware. I simply don’t have the kind of modern machine needed to render a full 3D short film comfortably.

So I started looking in a different direction: older software and hardware.

That’s when I found Maya 7, from the time when Maya was still made by Alias. You could actually buy the software and own it. No subscriptions. No logins. Just software that runs.

Of course, software from that era doesn’t run nicely on modern systems. So after a lot of research, I started looking for hardware from around 2005 — what I personally think of as a golden era of 3D animation production.

And surprisingly, you can still find incredibly powerful machines from that time for very reasonable prices — dual Xeon CPUs, Quadro graphics cards, massive amounts of RAM.

Yes, by modern standards this hardware is obsolete.

But movies like Monsters, Inc. and Toy Story were made with tools from this era. And they still look amazing today.

So that’s where the idea was born:

Build a retro animation workstation, make it run properly, and use it to create my film.
